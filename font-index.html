A Quick Read For Those in the Know

NO "CHILLING" HERE, WE WATCH THE 'FLIX TO GET INSPIRED

Mood boards for inspiration have been a staple in the
photography industry for decades. Nowadays, we have
so much visual inspiration at our fingertips. We took the
liberty to start your go-to list for visually tantalizing shows
on Netflix.

THE VISUAL STRATOSPHERE OF

NETFLIX

THE CROWN
This Netflix Original is one example of a show
whose cinematography and direction, in my
opinion, far outshines actual plot intrigue. The
show follows the life of Queen Elizabeth, which
may interest some, but--even though the acting
is spectacular--others, myself included, recoil at
the thought of watching a British history piece.
That said, I'm a huge fan, and it's because of
the show's incredible cinematography which
captures a consistently delightful tone, somehow
as soft and graceful as it is, in a sense, eerie.
Cinematographer Adriano Goldman steers clear
of bold color in most cases, opting for a clean
look that is concise and regal, as it should be.
this results in an almost ghostly cinema style
that exudes elegance.
STRANGER THINGS
The sci-fi smash hit's solid cinematography, the work of Tim Ives is perhaps
the key ingredient in establishing the Stranger Things' overall tone, one which
captures that unmissable 80's nostalgia but in a modern and vibrant way. Ives
hits the nail on the head in offering a depiction of suburban life that's true to it's
time--aside from the monsters and parallel worlds--in a relatively lo-fi quality,
but withstyle and color that only today's technological advancements could
allow. Be it the Byer's dullish house entwined in radiant Christmas lights or
the local arcade with it's striking mix of color, Stranger Things' cinematography
allows for an accurate, thoroughly enjoyable decade piece that's in no way
lacking in modernity.
ALTERED CARBON
Generally, in television, when a show is set in
a technologically rampant future, maintaining
a believable and consistent visual quality on a
grand scale tends to be rather difficult due to
budget constraints. Altered Carbon, however,
breaks that trend. The world manages to
feel lived-in and real, with CGI enhancing the
experience rather than distracting from it. The
cinematography meanwhile blends the darkness
of a gritty reality with a colorful popping neon
future, creating a visual feast that immerses
the viewer completely. Though the general
consensus is that Netflix's show is worth
watching, those on the fence might be swayed
to check it out just to see the impressive visuals.
THE END OF THE FUCKING WORLD
Directed by Jonathan Entwistle and Lucy
Tcherniak, this mini-series is based off of a
graphic novel. The story of a psychopath and a
loudmouth, the show takes notes from movies
like Fargo and Twin Peaks in the way that it is
visually constructed. The cinematographers
experiment with highly contrasted colors
and intense angles, especially when the
characters are in trouble or are about to
be. The establishing shot of each episode
typically features the main characters, James
and Alyssa, in the center of the frame. Their
emotions set the mood for the rest of the scene.
They are consistently framed by symmetrical
backgrounds--a gentle reminder that the rest
of the world is painfully normal as they move
along their suburban-noir quest.
